How to play

You are Coral. Rival species spread across the same living-chemistry field. Hold the most territory to clear each level — they get tougher as you climb.

  • Left-drag the field to seed your pattern; it grows and claims ground.
  • The Nutrient meter (top-left) limits how much you can seed: seeding drains it, and it refills over time — faster the more territory you hold. You can't spam — pick your spots.
  • Seed open ground ahead of the fronts to out-claim the AI. Brush S / M / L — bigger stamp, more nutrient.
  • Scoring: most points for clearing a level holding ~50% (a tight win beats a steamroll), plus a bonus for nutrient left over — so don't over-spend. Harder difficulty multiplies your score (Easy ×1, Normal ×2, Hard ×4).
  • You start each level with less nutrient than the last (and less on harder difficulty) — the squeeze tightens as you climb.
  • Clear a level by holding the most territory when time runs out (or reaching the win %). Higher levels — and harder difficulty — bring faster, then more, opponents.

How it works

Bloom is a deliberate showcase of WebGPU compute: the entire world — a grid of ~¼ million cells (over a million at higher grid sizes) — is simulated and scored on your graphics card, every frame. The CPU only injects your brush strokes and reads a tiny score back a few times a second.

  • The field is chemistry. Every cell holds two reacting chemicals evolving by the Gray-Scott reaction-diffusion equations — that's what produces the living spots, worms and branches, and why patterns spread, starve their own interiors, and form fighting fronts.
  • Factions are recipes. Each species is a different feed/kill pair, so they grow with genuinely different shapes and behaviour — the "meta" lives in the chemistry, not in scripted units.
  • Compute shaders advance all cells in parallel each frame; ownership & combat (who holds a cell, whose front pushes) are resolved per-cell on the GPU as well.
  • Scoring is a GPU reduction. Territory per faction is tallied on the card and only a tiny buffer (~16 KB) is read back ~3×/second — never the whole field. That keeps the entire game loop GPU-resident.
  • No build step. One self-contained HTML file using three.js r0.184 + TSL (Three Shading Language) loaded from a CDN, served as a static page on GitHub Pages.

Why WebGPU: compute shaders, storage buffers and on-GPU reduction make this possible — WebGL2 has none of them. It's a game that deliberately can't exist without GPU compute.

Bloom needs WebGPU

This game simulates a million-cell chemistry field on the GPU with compute shaders. Your browser doesn't expose WebGPU, so it can't run.

Use a recent Chrome or Edge (WebGPU on by default), or Firefox / Safari 18+. On desktop, make sure hardware acceleration is enabled.
